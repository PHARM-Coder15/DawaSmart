Red Eye Management Algorithm (WHO‑based)
  • Purulent discharge + sticky lids → Bacterial conjunctivitis → Topical chloramphenicol / gentamicin q2‑4h ×5‑7d.
  • Itching + watery discharge + bilateral → Allergic conjunctivitis → Antihistamine drops (olopatadine) + cold compresses.
  • Pain + photophobia + no discharge → Anterior uveitis / keratitis → Urgent ophthalmology referral; topical steroids only after specialist.
  • Foreign body sensation + tearing → Corneal abrasion → Fluorescein stain, topical antibiotic, patch if small; refer if large or infected.
Common Skin Conditions – Pathophysiology & Management

Acne Vulgaris

Pathophysiology: Follicular hyperkeratinisation, seborrhoea, Cutibacterium acnes, inflammation.
Mild: Benzoyl peroxide 2.5‑5% or topical adapalene. Moderate: Add topical clindamycin. Severe: Oral doxycycline 100mg bd + topical combination. Counsel: avoid picking, use non‑comedogenic products, expect 6‑8 weeks for improvement.

Leeds scale

Atopic Eczema

Pathophysiology: Skin barrier defect, immune dysregulation, environmental triggers.
Management ladder: Emollients (250‑500g/week) → mild topical corticosteroids (hydrocortisone) for face/folds → moderate (betamethasone valerate) for trunk/limbs. Antihistamines for itch. Avoid irritants.

Video: Emollient application

Herpes Zoster (Shingles)

Pathophysiology: Reactivation of varicella‑zoster virus in dorsal root ganglia.
Treatment: Oral acyclovir 800mg 5x/day ×7 days (within 72h). Analgesics (paracetamol, NSAIDs). Eye involvement (nasociliary sign) → emergency ophthalmology.

Dermatomal distribution

Fungal Infections (Tinea)

Types: Tinea corporis, cruris, pedis, capitis.
Treatment: Topical terbinafine 1% cream bd ×1‑2 weeks. Extensive or nail involvement: oral terbinafine 250mg od ×6‑12 weeks. Keep affected area dry, wash bedding.

STI Syndromic Approach (Tanzania National Guidelines)

Urethral Discharge

Likely pathogens: Neisseria gonorrhoeae, Chlamydia trachomatis.
Treatment: Ceftriaxone 250mg IM + Azithromycin 1g PO (directly observed). Partner management essential. Test for HIV, syphilis.

Vaginal Discharge

Trichomoniasis: Metronidazole 2g stat.
BV: Metronidazole 400mg bd ×7d.
Candidiasis: Clotrimazole 500mg pessary + topical cream. Advise to avoid douching.

Genital Ulcer

Common: Herpes simplex, syphilis, chancroid.
Treatment: Benzathine penicillin 2.4MU IM (syphilis) + acyclovir 400mg tds ×7d (herpes). RPR/VDRL, HIV testing.

Pelvic Inflammatory Disease (PID)

Outpatient regimen: Doxycycline 100mg bd ×14d + metronidazole 400mg bd ×14d + ceftriaxone 250mg IM stat.
Hospitalise if pregnancy, severe pain, or failed outpatient therapy.

Prevention & Counselling: Always offer HIV testing, condoms, and partner notification. Treat partners to prevent reinfection.
Minor Wound Management – Step‑by‑Step Evidence‑Based Protocol
  1. Assessment: Mechanism, size, depth, foreign body, neurovascular status, tetanus immunization history.
  2. Hemostasis: Direct pressure with sterile gauze for 5‑10 minutes.
  3. Cleaning: Irrigation with normal saline or potable water under pressure (10‑20mL/cm). Remove visible debris.
  4. Debridement: Remove devitalised tissue with scalpel or scissors (if trained).
  5. Antibiotics: Topical antiseptic (povidone‑iodine) only for contaminated wounds. Systemic antibiotics if high risk (diabetic, bite wounds, deep puncture).
  6. Dressing: Non‑adherent dressing, change daily or when soiled.
  7. Tetanus Prophylaxis: If dirty wound and greater than 5 years since last booster → Td vaccine; if unknown or greater than10 years → Td + immunoglobulin.

Bite wounds: high risk of infection; consider amoxicillin‑clavulanate prophylaxis; rabies risk assessment.
Venepuncture, Cannulation & Injection Techniques – Standard of Care

IV Cannulation

  • Select vein (forearm, hand, avoid valves).
  • Apply tourniquet, palpate.
  • Clean with chlorhexidine 70% alcohol, allow to dry.
  • Insert bevel up at 15‑30°, observe flashback, advance catheter, retract needle, secure.
  • Flush with 5‑10mL normal saline.
Video: Step‑by‑step

Intramuscular (IM) Injection

Sites: deltoid (adults), vastus lateralis (infants). Needle: 21‑23G, 1‑1.5 inch. Technique: stretch skin (Z‑track for irritant drugs), insert at 90°, aspirate, inject slowly.

Intradermal (ID) Injection

Angle 10‑15°, bevel up, inject 0.1mL to form bleb (tuberculin test, allergy testing).

Subcutaneous (SC) Injection

Pinch skin, insert 45°, no aspiration (insulin, heparin). Rotate sites to avoid lipohypertrophy.

Medical Devices – Correct Use & Maintenance

Metered‑Dose Inhaler (MDI)

Steps: Shake, exhale, place mouthpiece, actuate during slow inhalation, hold breath 10 sec. Use spacer for children/elderly. Clean mouthpiece weekly.

Video: Correct technique

Blood Pressure Monitor

Correct cuff size (arm circumference), rest 5 min, arm at heart level, no talking, measure twice. Record both systolic and diastolic.

Glucometer

Clean finger, first drop wipe, apply blood to strip, read. Store strips in cool, dry place; check expiry.

Nebulizer

Assemble, add prescribed solution, attach mask/mouthpiece, run until mist stops, clean after each use.

Integrated Clinical Cases (with Visual References)

Case 1: 8‑year‑old with itchy, scaly scalp, hair loss

Likely: Tinea capitis. Diagnosis: KOH microscopy, Wood’s lamp. Treatment: Oral terbinafine (weight‑based: 10kg = 125mg/day) + selenium sulfide shampoo twice weekly. Educate on avoiding sharing combs, hats.

Scalp fungal infection

Case 2: 32‑year‑old male, painful urination, purulent urethral discharge

Syndrome: Urethral discharge. Management: Ceftriaxone 250mg IM + azithromycin 1g PO. Test for HIV, syphilis. Advise partner treatment and abstinence until cured.

Case 3: 45‑year‑old diabetic with non‑healing foot ulcer, erythema, purulence

Assessment: Infected diabetic foot ulcer. Treatment: Debridement, wound culture, empiric amoxicillin‑clavulanate or clindamycin + ciprofloxacin. Offloading, tight glycaemic control, refer to podiatry. Monitor for osteomyelitis.

Wagner classification

Case 4: 60‑year‑old, sudden vision loss in one eye, painless, “curtain” over vision

Emergency: Retinal artery occlusion / retinal detachment. Immediate ophthalmology referral. Do not delay – time‑sensitive for salvage of vision.
