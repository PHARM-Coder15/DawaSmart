Masking Unpleasant Taste
One of reasons for compounding is to mask unpleasant taste of oral medicines. Explain five (5) approaches used to change bad tasting medicine.
  • Flavoring agents: Adding sweeteners or flavors (sugar, syrup, artificial flavors).
  • Coating: Covering solid dosage forms with a tasteless or sweet polymer film (e.g., sugar‑coated tablets).
  • Encapsulation: Enclosing the drug in soft gelatin capsules or microcapsules so it is released only in the stomach.
  • Multiple emulsion: Using a complex emulsion system to reduce contact with taste buds.
  • Complexation: Forming complexes with other substances to reduce interaction with taste receptors.
  • Effervescent agents: Formulations that release CO₂, creating a pleasant fizz that masks bitterness.

What is creaming and what are its possible reasons?

Definition: Upward movement of dispersed globules to form a thick layer at the surface of the emulsion.

Possible reasons:

@ Difference in density between phases;

@ inadequate viscosity or insufficient emulsifier.

What is cracking and what are its possible reasons?

Definition: Separation of two layers of disperse and continuous phase (irreversible).

Possible reasons:

@ Addition of emulsifying agent of opposite type;

@ decomposition or precipitation of emulgent;

@ addition of common solvent;

@ microbial action on emulsifying agent;

@ incorporation of excess disperse phase.

What is phase inversion and what causes it?

Definition: Change of one type of emulsion into the other type (e.g., oil-in-water changes to water-in-oil and vice versa).

Possible reasons:

@ Changing solubility of the emulgent;

@ addition of electrolytes;

@ changing the phase volume ratio;

@ temperature change;

@ changing the emulgent;

@ use of dirty equipment or incorrect method of preparation.

Explain the purpose of the following quality control test for tablets
Friability test.
To measure the mechanical strength of tablets and their ability to resist abrasion during handling, packaging, and transportation. Ensures durability and minimizes product loss.
Disintegration test.
To determine the time required for a tablet to break down into smaller particles and release its active ingredients when placed in a liquid medium under controlled conditions. Ensures that tablets disintegrate properly in the GI tract for effective drug absorption.
Hardness test.
To assess tablet resistance to breaking or crushing under pressure. Ensures that a tablet is robust enough for handling but not so hard that it fails to disintegrate or dissolve properly.
Dissolution test.
To measure the rate and extent of drug release from a tablet into a liquid medium under standardized conditions. Ensures the drug is available for absorption at the required rate, meeting therapeutic objectives.
Assay test.
To quantify the amount of active pharmaceutical ingredient present in a tablet compared to the label claim. Confirms the tablet contains the correct drug dosage and ensures consistency between batches.

Iodine Solution Preparation
Iodine is practically insoluble in water; briefly explain by showing chemical reaction involved how it is possible to make Iodine solution during compounding?

Iodine is practically insoluble in water but it can be made soluble by forming a complex with potassium iodide (KI). During compounding iodine reacts with potassium iodide to form a soluble triiodide ion (I3) which can dissolve in water.

Reaction: I₂ + KI → K⁺ + I₃⁻

Potassium iodide dissociates in water to form K⁺ and I⁻ ions.

@ Molecular iodine which is insoluble in water, reacts with the iodide ion to form triiodide ion which is water soluble thus create a clear and stable iodine solution.

Differentiate between the following


Solution and suspension
Solution; is a homogenous mixture where the solute is completely dissolved in the solvent WHILE Suspension; is a heterogamous mixture where solid particles are dispersed in liquid but not dissolved.
Emulsion and cream
Emulsion;Is one of heterogeneous liquid dosage forms, a two phase liquid system of immiscible liquids (water and oil) used orally WHILE, Cream; is a semisolid emulsion with higher viscosity used topically
Elixir and syrup
Elixir; is a clear, sweetened, hydro alcoholic solution containing medicinal substances WHILE Syrup; is a concentrated aqueous solution of sucrose containing medicinal products
Effervescent tablets and solution tablets
Effervescent tablets; contain acid and carbonate salt that release carbon dioxide when dissolved in water WHILE Solution tablet;dissolve directly in water without effervescence
Suppositories and enemas
Suppositories; are solid dosage forms inserted into the rectum to melt and release the drug WHILE Enemas; are liquid preparations introduced into the rectum for local or systemic effects.
Applications and lotion
Application are liquid or semi-solid preparations for application to the skin they may be solutions, emulsions or suspensions WHILE Lotion; these are liquid preparations intended for application to the unbroken skin to treat skin diseases or as antiseptics, antipyretics or protective. Solved Calculation Examples
Digoxin Liquid Conversion

Scenario: 11‑month‑old boy requires digoxin 0.0625mg PO once daily for 30 days. Mother reports poor compliance. Change to liquid (0.0625mg/5mL).

a) Total mg needed: 0.0625mg × 30 = 1.875mg
b) Volume to prepare: (1.875mg × 5mL) ÷ 0.0625mg = 150mL
c) 0.25mg tablets to use: 1.875mg ÷ 0.25mg = 7.5 → round up to 8 tablets

Dextrose 5% 1000mL with KCl – IV infusion

Scenario: 40 mEq KCl in 1000mL, 15 drops/mL, runs at 12 drops/min for 15 hours.

a) mEq KCl administered: Total drops = 12×900 = 10,800 → Volume = 10,800÷15 = 720mL → (40mEq/1000mL)×720 = 28.8 mEq
b) Grams KCl: 1 mEq KCl = 0.0745g → 28.8 × 0.0745 = 2.1456g

Drug H for George

Scenario: George 50kg, dose 4mg/kg/day in 4 divided doses. Available 25mg capsules.
